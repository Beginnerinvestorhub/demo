# CHAPTER 13
## Retirement Planning

> **Chapter Overview** — Retirement planning is the most consequential long-term financial project most people will undertake — and also the most commonly delayed. The math is unforgiving: every decade you wait roughly doubles the monthly savings required to reach the same retirement target. But the same math works in reverse for those who start early. This chapter provides the complete framework for retirement planning at every stage of life: choosing the right accounts, maximizing every tax advantage, calculating how much you actually need, navigating Social Security strategically, and building a withdrawal strategy designed to last as long as you do.

---

## 13.1 Retirement Planning Timeline

A retirement plan isn't a single decision — it's a series of increasingly specific decisions made over decades. The timeline below maps the key milestones and priorities at each stage of the journey.

### Retirement Planning by Decade

| Life Stage | Age | Key Priorities | Critical Actions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** | 20s | Start early; build habits | Open Roth IRA; contribute to 401(k) match minimum; establish emergency fund |
| **Acceleration** | 30s | Increase savings rate; optimize accounts | Max Roth IRA; increase 401(k) to maximum if possible; begin taxable investing |
| **Peak accumulation** | 40s | Maximize all tax-advantaged accounts | Max all accounts; consider HSA as retirement vehicle; review allocation |
| **Pre-retirement** | 50s | Assess trajectory; use catch-up contributions | Catch-up contributions ($7,500 extra in 401k, $1,000 in IRA); run retirement projections; plan Social Security timing |
| **Transition** | Early 60s | Finalize withdrawal strategy; Medicare planning | Coordinate Social Security filing; Medicare at 65; Roth conversion opportunities; sequence-of-returns risk management |
| **Distribution** | 65+ | Execute withdrawal strategy; manage RMDs | Follow withdrawal plan; take RMDs from traditional accounts at 73; manage taxes in retirement |

### Retirement Savings Benchmarks by Age

These are general guidelines — not guarantees — based on retiring at 65 with ~80% income replacement:

| Age | Suggested Savings Target | Based On |
|---|---|---|
| 30 | 1× annual salary | Starting strong |
| 35 | 2× annual salary | Compounding building |
| 40 | 3× annual salary | Peak accumulation beginning |
| 45 | 4× annual salary | Halfway point |
| 50 | 6× annual salary | Final stretch begins |
| 55 | 7× annual salary | Catch-up window |
| 60 | 8× annual salary | Near finish line |
| 67 | 10× annual salary | Full retirement target |

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — These benchmarks assume consistent investing from your 20s. If you're behind, don't panic — catch-up contributions, working a few extra years, or reducing planned retirement spending can each dramatically close the gap. What matters most is making a realistic assessment now and adjusting rather than avoiding the calculation.

## 13.2 401(k) Plans Explained

A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement savings plan that allows employees to contribute pre-tax income, reducing their taxable income today while investments grow tax-deferred until withdrawal. It is the primary retirement savings vehicle for most American workers.

### 401(k) Fast Facts (2025)

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Employee contribution limit | $23,500/year |
| Catch-up contribution (age 50+) | Additional $7,500/year ($31,000 total) |
| Super catch-up (age 60–63) | Additional $11,250/year ($34,750 total) under SECURE 2.0 |
| Total limit including employer contributions | $70,000/year |
| Tax treatment (Traditional) | Pre-tax contributions; taxable withdrawals |
| Tax treatment (Roth 401k) | After-tax contributions; tax-free withdrawals |
| Early withdrawal penalty | 10% + income tax if before age 59½ |
| Required beginning age for RMDs | Age 73 |

### Traditional 401(k) vs. Roth 401(k)

| Feature | Traditional 401(k) | Roth 401(k) |
|---|---|---|
| Contribution tax treatment | Pre-tax — reduces current taxable income | After-tax — no current tax benefit |
| Withdrawal tax treatment | Fully taxable as ordinary income | Tax-free (contributions + earnings) |
| Best when | You expect lower tax rate in retirement than today | You expect higher tax rate in retirement than today |
| RMDs required | Yes — at age 73 | No longer required (SECURE 2.0, effective 2024) |
| Income limits | None | None |

> 💡 **The 401(k) Employer Match** — Employer matching contributions are literally free money with an immediate 50–100% return before a single investment gains a penny. A 50% match on up to 6% of salary means contributing 6% and receiving the equivalent of 9%. Never, under any circumstances, contribute less than the amount required to receive the full employer match — it is the highest guaranteed return available in personal finance.

### Vesting Schedules — What You Actually Own

Employer contributions often vest over time — meaning you only keep them if you stay long enough:

| Vesting Type | How It Works | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediate vesting** | Employer match is yours from day one | None — most favorable to employees |
| **Cliff vesting** | 0% until a date, then 100% (e.g., 3-year cliff) | Leave before cliff = lose all employer contributions |
| **Graded vesting** | Percentage increases annually (e.g., 20%/year over 5 years) | Leave early = forfeit unvested portion |

## 13.3 Traditional IRA vs. Roth IRA

Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs) are personal retirement accounts — not tied to an employer — that provide tax advantages for long-term investing. The choice between Traditional and Roth is one of the most consequential tax decisions an investor makes.

### Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | Traditional IRA | Roth IRA |
|---|---|---|
| Contribution limit (2025) | $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+) | $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+) |
| Tax deductibility | Yes — if income is below phase-out threshold | No — contributions are after-tax |
| Income limit for contributions | No limit (but deductibility phases out) | Phase-out: $150K–$165K (single); $236K–$246K (married, 2025) |
| Investment growth | Tax-deferred | Tax-free |
| Withdrawals | Taxed as ordinary income | Tax-free (after age 59½; account 5+ years old) |
| Early withdrawal | 10% penalty + income tax | Contributions can be withdrawn penalty-free anytime; earnings have restrictions |
| Required Minimum Distributions | Yes — starting at age 73 | None during owner's lifetime |
| Best for | Higher earners wanting current deduction; those expecting lower taxes in retirement | Younger/lower earners expecting higher future taxes; those wanting tax flexibility |

### The Backdoor Roth IRA

For high earners above the Roth IRA income limits, the **backdoor Roth** strategy enables Roth contributions indirectly:

1. Make a non-deductible contribution to a Traditional IRA (no income limit)
2. Convert the Traditional IRA to a Roth IRA
3. Pay tax on any gains; the original contribution converts tax-free

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — The Roth IRA is widely considered the most powerful retirement account available to younger earners. Tax-free growth for 30–40 years on contributions made in low-tax years is extraordinary. A 25-year-old maxing a Roth IRA at $7,000/year for 40 years could accumulate $1.4M+ in completely tax-free wealth at a 7% return — never paying taxes on those gains regardless of future tax rates.

## 13.4 SEP IRA and Solo 401(k)

Self-employed individuals and small business owners have access to retirement accounts with dramatically higher contribution limits than traditional employee accounts — making these vehicles essential for anyone who controls their own income.

### Self-Employed Retirement Account Comparison

| Feature | SEP IRA | Solo 401(k) |
|---|---|---|
| Who qualifies | Any self-employed person or small business owner with employees | Business owners/self-employed with NO full-time employees (spouse exception) |
| 2025 contribution limit | Up to 25% of compensation, max $70,000 | Up to $70,000 ($77,500 with catch-up); includes employee + employer contributions |
| Employee contribution | No — employer contributions only | Yes — up to $23,500 as employee |
| Roth option | No | Yes — Roth Solo 401(k) available at most brokers |
| Setup complexity | Very simple | Moderate — requires plan documents |
| Loan provision | No | Yes — can borrow from Solo 401(k) |
| Best for | Sole proprietors wanting simplicity | Those wanting maximum contributions + Roth option + loan flexibility |

> 💡 **The Self-Employment Advantage** — A self-employed person earning $150,000/year can contribute up to $37,500 to a SEP IRA (25% of net self-employment income) — five times more than the standard IRA limit. A Solo 401(k) can go even higher by combining employee and employer contributions. High-earning self-employed individuals who don't use these accounts are leaving enormous tax advantages unclaimed.

## 13.5 Employer Matching Strategies

Employer matching is the closest thing to guaranteed investment return that exists — and optimizing it is the first and most important step in any retirement plan.

### Common Match Structures

| Match Type | How It Works | Your Cost / Employer Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Dollar-for-dollar match up to 3% | Employer matches every dollar you contribute, up to 3% of salary | You put in 3% → You get 3% free |
| 50% match up to 6% | Employer contributes 50 cents per dollar you contribute, up to 6% of salary | You put in 6% → You get 3% free |
| Tiered match | 100% on first 3% + 50% on next 2% | You put in 5% → You get 4% free |
| Annual discretionary match | Employer decides contribution amount each year | Unpredictable; don't rely on it for planning |

### Maximizing Your Match — The Strategy

- **Contribute at least the match threshold** before anything else — this is a 50–100% instant return
- **Understand vesting before job changes** — leaving before full vesting means forfeiting employer contributions
- **Don't count employer match against your contribution limit** — the $23,500 employee limit is separate from employer contributions
- **If you can't afford to max your 401(k)**, increase contributions by 1% per year — many plans have automatic annual escalation features

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — An employee earning $80,000 with a 50% match on up to 6% of salary who contributes 6% ($4,800) receives an additional $2,400 from their employer — a guaranteed 50% immediate return before the money is invested a single day. Over 30 years with 7% growth, that $2,400/year in "free" employer contributions alone compounds to approximately $242,000. Never leave this on the table.

## 13.6 Calculating Retirement Needs

How much do you actually need to retire? The answer depends on your lifestyle, health, longevity, and expected income from Social Security and other sources. The calculation is simpler than most people think.

### The 4% Rule — The Foundational Framework

The 4% rule (derived from the Trinity Study) states that a retiree can withdraw 4% of their initial portfolio in year one, adjust for inflation annually, and have a high probability of the portfolio lasting 30 years.

**Retirement Portfolio Target = Annual Retirement Spending ÷ 0.04 (or × 25)**

| Annual Retirement Spending | Portfolio Needed (4% Rule) |
|---|---|
| $40,000/year | $1,000,000 |
| $60,000/year | $1,500,000 |
| $80,000/year | $2,000,000 |
| $100,000/year | $2,500,000 |
| $120,000/year | $3,000,000 |

### Refining the Calculation

The 4% rule is a starting point. Adjust for:

| Factor | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| Retire early (before 65) | Use 3–3.5% withdrawal rate — longer timeline increases sequence risk |
| Retire late (after 67) | 4–4.5% may be sustainable — shorter expected retirement |
| Social Security reduces spending need | Calculate SS benefit and subtract from annual spending target before applying the 25× formula |
| High healthcare costs | Budget explicitly for Medicare premiums, supplements, and out-of-pocket costs |
| Want to leave inheritance | Build in additional buffer — 25× may not be enough |
| Flexible spending (can cut 10–20% in bear markets) | Can safely use 4–4.5% withdrawal rate |

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — Social Security fundamentally changes the math. If you'll receive $24,000/year in Social Security benefits and need $60,000/year to live, your portfolio only needs to generate $36,000/year — requiring $900,000 rather than $1,500,000. Maximizing your Social Security benefit (Section 13.7) can reduce your required portfolio by hundreds of thousands of dollars.

## 13.7 Social Security Basics

Social Security is a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted, lifetime income stream — one of the most valuable assets in a retirement plan. Yet most Americans claim it suboptimally, leaving tens of thousands of dollars in lifetime benefits unrealized.

### How Benefits Are Calculated

Your benefit is based on your highest 35 years of indexed earnings. If you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are averaged in — incentivizing those with work gaps to work longer.

**Full Retirement Age (FRA):** For those born in 1960 or later, FRA is age 67.

### The Claiming Age Impact

| Claiming Age | Monthly Benefit vs. FRA | Lifetime Impact |
|---|---|---|
| 62 (earliest) | −30% permanent reduction | Breakeven vs. age 67 claiming: ~age 79 |
| 65 | −13.3% reduction | Common choice for those needing income |
| 67 (FRA) | 100% — baseline benefit | The reference point |
| 70 (maximum) | +24% permanent increase | Breakeven vs. age 67 claiming: ~age 82 |

### Optimal Claiming Strategies

| Situation | Recommended Strategy |
|---|---|
| Single, good health, expects to live past 82 | Delay to 70 — maximizes lifetime total |
| Single, poor health or shortened life expectancy | Claim early — collect more years even at reduced rate |
| Married couple, significant income difference | Higher earner delays to 70; lower earner may claim earlier |
| Need income at 62, no other options | Claim early rather than depleting portfolio |
| Still working at 62–FRA | Avoid claiming while working — benefit may be reduced by earnings test |

> 💡 **The Spousal Benefit** — A spouse who never worked (or earned significantly less) is entitled to up to 50% of the higher-earning spouse's FRA benefit. The higher earner delaying to 70 also increases the survivor benefit — the amount the surviving spouse receives after the first spouse dies. For married couples, Social Security optimization is a two-person strategy with significant lifetime value at stake.

## 13.8 Pension Plans

Defined benefit pension plans — once the standard in American employment — guarantee a specific monthly income in retirement based on a formula, regardless of investment market performance. While now rare in the private sector, they remain common in government, military, and some union jobs.

### Defined Benefit vs. Defined Contribution

| Feature | Defined Benefit (Pension) | Defined Contribution (401k/IRA) |
|---|---|---|
| Who bears investment risk | Employer | Employee |
| Retirement income | Guaranteed monthly payment for life | Depends on investment performance and withdrawal rate |
| Portability | Limited — often reduces with early departure | Fully portable; roll over to new plan or IRA |
| Inflation adjustment | Varies — some include COLA; many don't | Investment returns provide inflation protection |
| Longevity protection | Yes — payments continue for life regardless | Risk of outliving the portfolio |
| Prevalence | ~20% of private sector; ~80%+ of government workers | Dominant in private sector |

### Pension Decision Point: Lump Sum vs. Annuity

Many retirees face a choice between taking a pension as a monthly annuity or a one-time lump sum:

- **Take the annuity if:** You value predictability, have no other guaranteed income, have a long life expectancy, or worry about managing a lump sum
- **Take the lump sum if:** You have significant other assets, shorter life expectancy, want investment control, or wish to leave an inheritance

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — Before leaving any job with a pension, request the full pension benefit statement and understand the vesting schedule, survivor options, and early retirement reduction factors. Many people dramatically underestimate the value of a pension annuity — a $2,500/month pension for life is equivalent to roughly a $750,000 lump sum at a 4% withdrawal rate. Don't trade this away without careful analysis.

## 13.9 Retirement Withdrawal Strategies

Accumulating wealth is only half the retirement challenge. Distributing it tax-efficiently over a 20–35 year retirement — without running out — requires an equally deliberate strategy.

### The Core Withdrawal Frameworks

| Strategy | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **4% Rule** | Withdraw 4% of initial portfolio in year 1; adjust annually for inflation | Simple baseline; most applicable for 30-year retirements |
| **Dynamic withdrawal** | Increase or decrease withdrawals based on portfolio performance | Flexible spenders who can cut back in bear markets |
| **Bucket strategy** | Divide portfolio into short-term (cash), medium-term (bonds), and long-term (stocks) buckets | Psychological comfort; ensures near-term spending isn't in volatile assets |
| **Floor and upside** | Annuitize essential spending needs; invest remainder for growth | Those prioritizing security over maximizing legacy |
| **Guardrails** | Set upper and lower spending limits tied to portfolio value | Balance between flexibility and sustainability |

### Tax-Efficient Withdrawal Sequencing

The order in which you draw from accounts dramatically affects lifetime tax liability:

| Step | Account Type | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Required Minimum Distributions (traditional accounts) | Mandatory — no choice after age 73 |
| 2 | Taxable brokerage accounts | Often most tax-efficient to spend first; step-up in basis at death |
| 3 | Traditional 401(k) / IRA | Taxable on withdrawal; deplete before Roth to manage tax brackets |
| 4 | Roth IRA / Roth 401(k) | Last — tax-free growth maximized; no RMDs; best for inheritance |

> 💡 **Roth Conversion Ladder** — The years between retirement and age 73 (when RMDs begin) often represent the lowest-tax window of a retiree's life. Converting traditional IRA funds to Roth each year — up to the top of your current tax bracket — can reduce future RMD amounts, lower lifetime taxes, and leave a larger tax-free inheritance. This strategy requires a financial planner to model effectively but can save six figures in lifetime taxes for many retirees.

## 13.10 Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

The IRS requires withdrawals from most tax-deferred retirement accounts beginning at age 73 (under SECURE 2.0 Act). Failing to take RMDs results in a 25% penalty on the amount not withdrawn — one of the harshest penalties in the tax code.

### RMD Rules at a Glance

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Starting age | 73 (born 1951–1959); 75 (born 1960 or later, starting 2033) |
| Accounts subject to RMDs | Traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b), SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA — most tax-deferred accounts |
| Accounts exempt from RMDs | Roth IRA (during owner's lifetime); Roth 401(k) (effective 2024) |
| RMD amount calculation | Prior year-end account balance ÷ IRS life expectancy factor |
| Penalty for missed RMD | 25% of the amount that should have been withdrawn (reduced to 10% if corrected quickly) |
| Can you take more than the minimum? | Yes — RMD is the floor, not the ceiling |
| Can you reinvest RMD proceeds? | Yes — after paying tax, move to taxable brokerage account |

### RMD Planning Strategies

- **Roth conversions before RMDs begin** — Reduce traditional account balances in low-tax years to minimize future RMD amounts
- **Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)** — Direct up to $105,000/year (2025) from IRA to charity; satisfies RMD without increasing taxable income
- **Aggregation rule** — Multiple traditional IRAs can aggregate RMDs and take from any single account; 401(k)s must be calculated and taken separately

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — RMDs can be a stealth tax bomb for retirees who deferred heavily into traditional accounts. A retiree with $2 million in a traditional IRA faces RMDs of $75,000+ annually at age 75 — potentially pushing them into higher tax brackets, increasing Medicare premiums (IRMAA surcharges), and reducing Social Security after-tax income. The Roth conversion window between retirement and age 73 is the critical opportunity to prevent this.
