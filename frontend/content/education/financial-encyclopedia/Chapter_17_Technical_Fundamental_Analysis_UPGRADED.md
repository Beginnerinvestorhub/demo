# CHAPTER 17
## Technical & Fundamental Analysis

> **Chapter Overview** — Every price on every market represents a collision between two forces: what a company is actually worth (fundamental analysis) and what participants believe it will do next (technical analysis). Understanding both gives you a complete picture of the investing process — one that separates informed decision-making from guesswork. Fundamental analysis asks "what is this worth?" Technical analysis asks "what is the market doing right now?" Neither is complete without the other. This chapter builds fluency in both: reading financial statements, applying valuation metrics, interpreting chart patterns, and using technical indicators to identify entry and exit opportunities with greater precision.

---

## 17.1 Fundamental Analysis Principles

Fundamental analysis evaluates a company's intrinsic value by examining its financial health, business model, competitive position, management quality, and the broader economic environment it operates in. The core belief: over time, a stock's price will converge with the company's true underlying value — making mispricing an exploitable opportunity for patient investors.

### The Three Layers of Fundamental Analysis

| Layer | What You're Analyzing | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Macroeconomic analysis** | Economy-wide trends affecting all businesses | Is GDP growing? Are interest rates rising or falling? Where are we in the business cycle? |
| **Industry / sector analysis** | Competitive dynamics and structural trends within a sector | Is the industry growing or shrinking? How intense is competition? What are the barriers to entry? |
| **Company analysis** | Individual business fundamentals | Is revenue growing? Are margins expanding? Is management allocating capital wisely? What is the moat? |

### What Makes a High-Quality Business — The Checklist

| Factor | Strong Signal | Warning Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue growth** | 10%+ consistently over 5+ years | Declining or highly volatile revenue |
| **Gross margin** | Expanding or stable at 40%+ (varies by industry) | Compressing margins — pricing power erosion |
| **Free cash flow** | Consistently positive; growing faster than earnings | Earnings without cash flow — may be accounting-driven |
| **Return on Equity (ROE)** | 15%+ sustainably | Declining ROE without explanation |
| **Debt levels** | Debt-to-equity < 1× for most industries | Excessive debt relative to cash flow (debt/EBITDA > 4×) |
| **Competitive moat** | Brand, network effect, switching costs, cost advantage, patents | Commoditized product with no differentiation |
| **Management quality** | High insider ownership; rational capital allocation; shareholder-friendly | Excessive dilution; empire-building acquisitions; poor disclosure |

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — The goal of fundamental analysis is not to predict short-term price movements — it's to estimate intrinsic value and identify when the market is offering you a security at a discount to that value. The greater the discount (margin of safety), the more attractive the investment. This process takes time and patience — but over full market cycles, the fundamentally grounded investor consistently outperforms those trading on news and sentiment.

## 17.2 Reading Financial Statements

Every publicly traded company files quarterly (10-Q) and annual (10-K) reports with the SEC. These contain three core financial statements that together tell the complete story of a business's financial health.

### The Three Financial Statements

**The Income Statement — Profitability Over Time**

| Line Item | What It Shows | Key Ratio Derived |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue (top line)** | Total sales of products and services | Revenue growth rate YoY |
| **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** | Direct costs of producing revenue | Gross margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue |
| **Gross profit** | Revenue minus COGS | Gross margin % — pricing power indicator |
| **Operating expenses** | SG&A, R&D, depreciation | Operating leverage |
| **Operating income (EBIT)** | Profit before interest and taxes | Operating margin % |
| **Net income (bottom line)** | Final profit after all costs, taxes, interest | Net margin; EPS = Net income ÷ shares |

**The Balance Sheet — Financial Position at a Moment in Time**

| Section | What It Shows | Key Metric |
|---|---|---|
| **Current assets** | Cash, receivables, inventory — convertible to cash in <1 year | Current ratio = Current assets ÷ Current liabilities |
| **Long-term assets** | Property, equipment, intangibles, goodwill | Asset turnover = Revenue ÷ Total assets |
| **Current liabilities** | Accounts payable, short-term debt — due within 1 year | Quick ratio = (Cash + Receivables) ÷ Current liabilities |
| **Long-term liabilities** | Long-term debt, deferred taxes | Debt-to-equity = Total debt ÷ Total equity |
| **Shareholders' equity** | Assets minus liabilities — book value | Return on equity = Net income ÷ Equity |

**The Cash Flow Statement — Where Money Actually Went**

| Section | What It Shows | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Operating cash flow** | Cash generated from core business operations | Most important section — the true earnings quality test |
| **Investing cash flow** | Cash spent on capital expenditures, acquisitions, investments | Negative is often healthy — investing in future growth |
| **Financing cash flow** | Cash from debt issuance/repayment, stock issuance/buybacks, dividends | Shows capital structure changes |
| **Free cash flow** | Operating cash flow − Capital expenditures | The money available for dividends, buybacks, debt reduction |

> 💡 **The Cash Flow Quality Test** — A company reporting high net earnings but generating little or negative operating cash flow is a red flag. High-quality earnings are backed by cash — net income and operating cash flow should move roughly in tandem over time. Wide divergence (high net income, low cash flow) often signals aggressive revenue recognition, channel stuffing, or unsustainable accounting practices.

## 17.3 P/E Ratios and Valuation Metrics

Valuation metrics answer the core question of fundamental analysis: relative to what the company earns, owns, or generates — how much are investors paying? These metrics make stocks comparable across size, sector, and time.

### The Core Valuation Metrics

| Metric | Formula | What It Measures | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **P/E (Price-to-Earnings)** | Share price ÷ EPS | How much you pay per dollar of earnings | Higher = more growth expected; lower = cheaper or declining |
| **Forward P/E** | Share price ÷ Estimated next-year EPS | Current price vs. expected future earnings | More forward-looking than trailing P/E |
| **PEG ratio** | P/E ÷ Annual earnings growth rate | P/E adjusted for growth | PEG < 1 = potentially undervalued given growth |
| **P/B (Price-to-Book)** | Share price ÷ Book value per share | Price vs. net asset value | Below 1 = trading below liquidation value |
| **P/S (Price-to-Sales)** | Market cap ÷ Annual revenue | Price vs. revenue | Useful for unprofitable growth companies |
| **EV/EBITDA** | Enterprise value ÷ EBITDA | Total company value vs. operating earnings | Better than P/E for comparing companies with different debt levels |
| **Dividend yield** | Annual dividend ÷ Share price | Income as % of price | Higher yield = more income; very high may signal risk |
| **Free cash flow yield** | Free cash flow ÷ Market cap | Cash generation vs. price | FCF yield > 5% often indicates value |

### Valuation Benchmarks — Context by Sector

| Sector | Typical P/E Range | Notable Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Technology | 25–50× | High growth expectations; often valued on revenue |
| Healthcare | 20–35× | Patent expiration risk; pipeline optionality |
| Consumer staples | 18–28× | Stable earnings; premium for reliability |
| Financials | 10–15× | Price-to-book often more relevant than P/E |
| Energy | 8–15× | Cyclical; earnings volatile with commodity prices |
| Utilities | 14–20× | Bond-like; rate-sensitive |
| S&P 500 historical average | ~16–18× trailing earnings | Long-run mean reversion baseline |

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — No valuation metric works in isolation. A P/E of 30× is cheap for a company growing earnings at 40%/year (PEG of 0.75) and wildly expensive for a company growing at 5%/year (PEG of 6.0). Always combine multiple metrics, compare to industry peers, and assess the growth trajectory. The best fundamental investors evaluate absolute value *and* relative value *and* the quality of the business behind the numbers.

## 17.4 Technical Analysis Introduction

Technical analysis studies historical price and volume data to identify patterns, trends, and momentum — with the goal of forecasting future price behavior and optimizing the timing of entries and exits. Unlike fundamental analysis, it is entirely agnostic about what a company is worth; it only cares about what the market is doing.

### The Three Core Assumptions of Technical Analysis

| Assumption | What It Means | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Markets discount everything** | All known information is already reflected in price | No need to analyze news or earnings — it's in the chart |
| **Prices move in trends** | Once a trend is established, it tends to continue until it reverses | Trade with the trend; don't fight momentum |
| **History repeats** | Investor psychology creates recurring patterns | The same chart patterns produce similar outcomes over time |

### Fundamental vs. Technical Analysis — A Comparison

| Factor | Fundamental Analysis | Technical Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| Primary question | What is this worth? | What will this do next? |
| Time horizon | Long-term (months to years) | Short-to-medium term (days to months) |
| Data used | Financial statements, economic data, competitive analysis | Price charts, volume, momentum indicators |
| Primary users | Long-term investors, value investors | Traders, short-term speculators, active managers |
| Limitations | Says nothing about timing; stocks can be cheap for years | Says nothing about intrinsic value; patterns aren't always reliable |
| Best combined with | Technical timing for entries/exits | Fundamental filter to ensure quality |

> 💡 **The Integration Approach** — The most effective practitioners use both: fundamental analysis to identify *what* to own (quality businesses at reasonable prices), and technical analysis to optimize *when* to buy (entering near support; avoiding buying into overhead resistance). Neither discipline alone is complete.

## 17.5 Chart Patterns

Chart patterns are recurring price formation sequences that technical analysts use to predict likely future price direction. They emerge from the collective psychology of buyers and sellers interacting at key price levels.

### Reversal Patterns — Signal a Trend Change

| Pattern | What It Looks Like | Signal | Reliability |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Head and Shoulders** | Three peaks — middle (head) is highest; two lower peaks (shoulders) | Bearish reversal — uptrend ending | High — one of the most reliable reversal patterns |
| **Inverse Head and Shoulders** | Three troughs — middle is lowest | Bullish reversal — downtrend ending | High |
| **Double Top** | Two peaks at similar price levels with pullback between | Bearish reversal — resistance confirmed | Moderate–High |
| **Double Bottom** | Two troughs at similar price levels | Bullish reversal — support confirmed | Moderate–High |
| **Rounding Bottom (Cup)** | Gradual U-shaped base over months | Bullish reversal — long base suggests strong breakout | High when volume confirms |

### Continuation Patterns — Signal Trend Resumption

| Pattern | What It Looks Like | Signal | Typical Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bull flag** | Sharp rally (flagpole) followed by tight sideways/down consolidation | Bullish continuation — pause before next leg up | Breakout equal to flagpole in height |
| **Bear flag** | Sharp decline followed by tight sideways/up consolidation | Bearish continuation — pause before next leg down | Breakdown equal to flagpole in depth |
| **Ascending triangle** | Flat resistance + rising support — coiling upward | Bullish continuation (usually) | Breakout above flat resistance |
| **Descending triangle** | Flat support + declining resistance — coiling downward | Bearish continuation (usually) | Breakdown below flat support |
| **Symmetrical triangle** | Converging trendlines — direction neutral until breakout | Continuation in direction of breakout | Watch for volume confirmation |
| **Pennant** | Brief tight consolidation after sharp move | Continuation of prior direction | Similar to flag; shorter consolidation |

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — Chart patterns are probabilistic, not deterministic. A head-and-shoulders pattern doesn't guarantee a decline — it shifts the probability. The reliability of any pattern increases significantly when confirmed by volume: a breakout on high volume is more trustworthy than one on thin trading. Always use patterns in conjunction with support/resistance levels and at least one confirming indicator.

## 17.6 Moving Averages

Moving averages smooth out price data by averaging prices over a defined period — filtering out short-term noise to reveal the underlying trend direction. They are among the most widely used and reliable technical tools available.

### Types of Moving Averages

| Type | Calculation | Characteristics | Best Used For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Simple Moving Average (SMA)** | Equal-weighted average of N closing prices | Smooth; slow to react | Identifying long-term trend direction |
| **Exponential Moving Average (EMA)** | Weighted average giving more weight to recent prices | Faster; more reactive | Shorter-term trading signals; momentum |
| **Weighted Moving Average (WMA)** | Linear weighting — most recent price weighted highest | Between SMA and EMA | Reducing lag while maintaining smoothness |

### The Most Widely Watched Moving Averages

| Moving Average | Significance | Trader Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **20-day SMA/EMA** | Short-term trend | Price above = short-term uptrend; below = short-term downtrend |
| **50-day SMA** | Medium-term trend | Major support/resistance level; widely watched by institutions |
| **200-day SMA** | Long-term trend | The single most important moving average; defines bull/bear trend |

### The Golden Cross and Death Cross

| Signal | What Happens | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Golden Cross** | 50-day SMA crosses **above** 200-day SMA | Bullish long-term signal — major uptrend beginning |
| **Death Cross** | 50-day SMA crosses **below** 200-day SMA | Bearish long-term signal — major downtrend beginning |

> 💡 **Moving Average Support and Resistance** — Major moving averages (50-day, 200-day) act as dynamic support in uptrends and dynamic resistance in downtrends. A stock repeatedly bouncing off its 50-day SMA in an uptrend is behaving as expected — each touch is a potential low-risk entry. A stock breaking decisively below its 200-day SMA on high volume is a major warning signal regardless of how strong its fundamentals appear.

## 17.7 RSI and MACD Indicators

Momentum indicators quantify the speed and strength of price movements — helping identify when assets are overbought (likely to pull back) or oversold (likely to bounce), and when trend momentum is accelerating or decelerating.

### Relative Strength Index (RSI)

The RSI measures the magnitude of recent price gains vs. recent losses on a 0–100 scale. It's calculated over a default 14-period lookback window.

| RSI Reading | Interpretation | Action Consideration |
|---|---|---|
| **Above 70** | Overbought — price has risen fast; momentum may slow | Consider trimming; avoid new longs |
| **50–70** | Bullish momentum — uptrend intact | Trend-following entries appropriate |
| **50** | Neutral — no clear directional momentum | Assess trend from other indicators |
| **30–50** | Bearish momentum — downtrend intact | Avoid longs; trend-following shorts |
| **Below 30** | Oversold — price has fallen fast; potential bounce | Consider covering shorts; watch for reversal signals |

**RSI Divergence — A Powerful Warning Signal:**
- **Bearish divergence:** Price makes a new high; RSI makes a lower high → weakening momentum despite rising price → potential reversal
- **Bullish divergence:** Price makes a new low; RSI makes a higher low → strengthening momentum despite falling price → potential reversal

### MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

MACD plots the relationship between two exponential moving averages (typically 12-day and 26-day EMAs) to identify trend direction and momentum changes.

| MACD Component | What It Is | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **MACD line** | 12-day EMA minus 26-day EMA | Positive = short-term EMA above long-term = uptrend |
| **Signal line** | 9-day EMA of MACD line | Smoothed version used for crossover signals |
| **Histogram** | MACD line minus Signal line | Shows momentum acceleration/deceleration |

| MACD Signal | What It Means |
|---|---|
| MACD crosses **above** signal line | Bullish — momentum shifting upward |
| MACD crosses **below** signal line | Bearish — momentum shifting downward |
| Histogram growing (positive) | Bullish momentum accelerating |
| Histogram shrinking (positive) | Bullish momentum decelerating — watch for reversal |
| MACD divergence from price | Same interpretation as RSI divergence — early warning of trend change |

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — RSI and MACD are most powerful when they agree with each other and with the price trend. A stock in a confirmed uptrend (above 200-day SMA), with RSI pulling back from overbought to ~50 (healthy reset), and MACD crossing bullish above the signal line — that convergence of signals provides significantly more confidence than any single indicator used alone. Never trade a single indicator in isolation.

## 17.8 Volume Analysis

Volume — the number of shares traded in a given period — is the fuel behind price movements. Price changes backed by high volume are more significant and more likely to persist; price changes on low volume are more likely to be false signals or short-lived.

### Volume as a Confirmation Tool

| Price + Volume Combination | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Price rising + Volume rising | Strong buying pressure — trend likely to continue |
| Price rising + Volume falling | Weakening buying interest — possible exhaustion; be cautious |
| Price falling + Volume rising | Strong selling pressure — trend likely to continue lower |
| Price falling + Volume falling | Seller exhaustion — potential base forming; watch for reversal |
| Breakout above resistance + High volume | High-conviction breakout — likely to hold |
| Breakout above resistance + Low volume | Suspect breakout — likely to fail and retest resistance |

### Volume Indicators

| Indicator | What It Measures | How to Use It |
|---|---|---|
| **On-Balance Volume (OBV)** | Cumulative volume — adds volume on up days, subtracts on down days | Rising OBV in uptrend confirms buying; OBV diverging from price warns of reversal |
| **Volume Moving Average** | Average daily volume over 20–50 days | Compare today's volume to average: 2× average = significant; 0.5× = light |
| **Volume at Price (VAP)** | Shows volume distribution at each price level | Identifies high-volume nodes (strong support/resistance) |

> 💡 **The Climax Volume Signal** — An exceptionally high-volume day (3–5× average) after a prolonged trend often signals a climax — either a buying climax (exhaustion of buyers; likely to reverse lower) or a selling climax (exhaustion of sellers; likely to reverse higher). These extreme volume events mark significant turning points that often set the tone for weeks of subsequent price action.

## 17.9 Support and Resistance Levels

Support and resistance are the most fundamental concepts in technical analysis — price levels where supply and demand have historically balanced, creating predictable decision points for buyers and sellers.

### Definitions and Characteristics

| Concept | Definition | Why It Forms |
|---|---|---|
| **Support** | A price level where buying interest has historically prevented further decline | Traders who missed the last rally place buy orders at the level; those who sold short cover positions |
| **Resistance** | A price level where selling pressure has historically prevented further advance | Traders who bought at the top and are "breakeven" sell to exit; short sellers add positions |
| **Role reversal** | When broken, support becomes new resistance and vice versa | The psychological reference level shifts — former buyers who are now underwater want out |

### How Support and Resistance Develop

| Source | Example | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| **Prior highs and lows** | A previous 52-week high that was rejected | Strong — widely watched by all participants |
| **Round numbers** | $100, $500, $1,000 price levels | Moderate — psychological anchoring effect |
| **Moving averages** | 200-day SMA acting as dynamic support | Moderate–Strong — institutional reference level |
| **Gap fills** | A price gap on a chart is often "filled" in subsequent trading | Moderate |
| **Fibonacci retracements** | 38.2%, 50%, 61.8% retracement of a prior move | Moderate — widely used by technical traders |

### Using Support and Resistance in Practice

| Application | How to Use It |
|---|---|
| **Entry timing** | Buy near support in an uptrend (lower risk); buy breakouts above resistance with volume confirmation |
| **Stop-loss placement** | Place stops just below support — if support breaks, the thesis is invalidated |
| **Profit targets** | Target the next resistance level above entry as a price objective |
| **Position sizing** | Tighter risk (support close to entry) allows larger position size for same dollar risk |

> ⚡ **Key Insight** — The most powerful trade setups occur when multiple technical factors converge at the same price level — for example, a stock pulling back to its 200-day SMA (support), which also coincides with a prior high (now acting as support after a breakout), while RSI resets to 40–50 and volume is declining (healthy consolidation). This convergence of support from multiple sources dramatically increases the probability of a bounce relative to any single factor alone.

## Chapter 17 Summary: Technical & Fundamental Analysis at a Glance

| Topic | Core Principle | Key Application |
|---|---|---|
| Fundamental Analysis | Estimate intrinsic value; buy at a discount | Three-layer analysis: macro → industry → company |
| Reading Financial Statements | Income statement = profitability; balance sheet = position; cash flow = reality | Always check FCF vs. net income for earnings quality |
| Valuation Metrics | No single metric sufficient — combine P/E, PEG, EV/EBITDA, FCF yield | Compare to sector peers and historical averages |
| Technical Analysis | Price and volume reveal collective market psychology | Use to time entries/exits after fundamental filter identifies *what* to own |
| Chart Patterns | Recurring formations reflect predictable human behavior | Require volume confirmation; treat as probabilistic, not certain |
| Moving Averages | Smooth price data to reveal trend direction | 200-day SMA = long-term trend; 50-day = medium-term; crossovers = signals |
| RSI & MACD | Momentum indicators identify overbought/oversold conditions and trend strength | Most powerful when multiple indicators agree; divergences are early warnings |
| Volume Analysis | Volume confirms or questions price moves | High volume on breakouts = conviction; low volume = suspect |
| Support & Resistance | Price levels where supply and demand have historically balanced | Multiple confluent levels = strongest signals; use for entries, stops, targets |
